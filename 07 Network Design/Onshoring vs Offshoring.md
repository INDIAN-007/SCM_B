# Onshoring vs Offshoring

> **Source:** Chopra Ch.6
> **Tags:** #network #global #sourcing

---

## Decision Framework

The make-or-buy, where-to-source decision involves evaluating:
- **Total Cost of Ownership** (not just unit price)
- Lead time impact
- Quality reliability
- IP risk
- Resilience / supply disruption risk
- Flexibility to ramp up/down

---

## Comparison

| Factor | Offshoring | Nearshoring | Onshoring |
|---|---|---|---|
| Labour cost | Lowest | Medium | Highest |
| Lead time | Longest | Medium | Shortest |
| Responsiveness | Low | Medium | High |
| Supply risk | High | Medium | Low |
| IP protection | Lowest | Medium | Highest |
| Flexibility | Low | Medium | High |
| Carbon footprint | High (long transport) | Medium | Low |

---

## When Offshoring Makes Sense
- Product is standard, high-volume, low customisation
- Lead time tolerance is long
- Quality can be verified reliably
- IP is not sensitive
- Unit cost difference is large enough to justify TCO

## When Onshoring / Nearshoring Makes Sense
- Product is customised or rapidly changing
- Response time is critical
- Quality must be tightly controlled
- IP is a competitive asset
- Geopolitical risk is high
- Customer or regulatory requires local content

---

## The Value of Flexibility (Chopra)

Using decision trees: the **option value of flexible manufacturing** (ability to shift production between regions) is particularly valuable in uncertain environments.

Example: A firm that can produce both in China and Mexico can switch based on which is more cost-effective as exchange rates and tariffs change.
