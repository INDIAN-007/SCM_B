# Global Supply Chains

> **Source:** Chopra Ch.6
> **Tags:** #network #global #international

---

## Impact of Globalization

Globalization enables:
- Access to lower-cost manufacturing (labour, materials)
- Access to new customer markets
- Geographic risk diversification

But introduces:
- Longer lead times (ocean shipping: weeks vs days)
- Greater supply uncertainty (political, weather, port disruptions)
- Currency risk
- Regulatory complexity (tariffs, customs, import duties)
- Coordination complexity

---

## Factors Influencing Network Design Decisions (Chopra Ch.5)

| Factor | Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Macroeconomic** | Tariffs, tax incentives, import/export duties, exchange rates |
| **Political stability** | Risk of nationalisation, conflict, regulatory changes |
| **Infrastructure** | Port quality, road/rail, power reliability |
| **Competitive** | Where competitors operate; cluster effects (Silicon Valley, Shenzhen) |
| **Proximity to customers** | Response time, local content requirements |
| **Labour** | Cost, skill level, availability, union environment |

## Global Supply Chain Risk Categories

| Risk Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Supply disruption** | Supplier failure, natural disaster, political crisis |
| **Demand disruption** | Recession, pandemic-driven demand shift |
| **Currency risk** | Exchange rate fluctuations affecting cost structure |
| **Regulatory risk** | Tariff changes, import bans, content requirements |
| **Intellectual property** | Technology theft in offshore manufacturing |
| **Quality risk** | Lower quality standards in offshore locations |

---

## Risk Mitigation Strategies

| Strategy | Description |
|---|---|
| **Dual/Multiple sourcing** | Qualified backup suppliers in different regions |
| **Flexible supply base** | Suppliers can shift volumes quickly |
| **Regional production** | Manufacturing closer to each major market |
| **Inventory buffer** | Higher safety stock for longer-lead-time components |
| **Financial hedging** | Currency forwards and options |
| **Supply chain mapping** | Visibility beyond Tier 1 to Tier 2/3 suppliers |

---

## Onshoring vs Offshoring vs Nearshoring

| Strategy | Description | Drivers |
|---|---|---|
| **Offshoring** | Move production to low-cost distant country | Labour cost, tax incentives |
| **Nearshoring** | Move production to nearby lower-cost country | Balance of cost + responsiveness |
| **Onshoring / Reshoring** | Bring production back home | Resilience, quality, IP protection, political |
| **Friendshoring** | Source from politically aligned countries | Geopolitical risk reduction |

## Decision Tree Approach (Chopra Ch.6)

For network decisions under uncertainty (e.g., demand, exchange rates), use **decision trees** to value the flexibility of different options.

Flexibility (e.g., the ability to shift production between plants) has **option value** — especially in volatile environments.

## Nokia Case (Chopra Ch.6)

Nokia's global supply chain design required balancing:
- Component sourcing from Asia
- Manufacturing in multiple regions
- Rapid response to fast-changing mobile market
- Risk of single-source component dependence

Lesson: Diversification across geography and suppliers is essential for resilient global SC.
