## Case Study: Frozen and Refrigerated Foods Cold Chain

A consortium of food retailers and suppliers studied the total cost of the cold chain across company boundaries. Finding:
- Each company had optimised its own piece of the cold chain
- The interfaces between companies were full of cost and risk
- Temperature excursions at handoff points were common but invisible
- Consolidated tracking and joint responsibility reduced excursions by 60%+

**Lesson:** Cost reduction across company boundaries requires collaboration — no one company can fix it alone.

---

## Recognising Root Causes Together

The first step of multicompany collaboration is a joint root cause analysis:
- Both buyer and supplier map the same process
- They identify which costs are driven by WHOM
- Often: "Your behaviour causes MY cost, and vice versa"

**Example:**
- Retailer promotes aggressively → order spikes → supplier carries buffer stock for peak → supplier charges for buffer in unit price
- Retailer shares promo calendar early → supplier plans production smoothly → no buffer needed → lower unit cost

## Who to Collaborate With — Rationalising the Supplier/Customer Base

Not all suppliers deserve deep collaboration:
- **Strategic suppliers** — high spend, differentiated product, critical to range → Stage 3 collaboration
- **Leverage suppliers** — high spend, commodity product → negotiate hard; some data sharing
- **Bottleneck suppliers** — low spend, unique product → relationship investment to secure supply
- **Routine suppliers** — low spend, commodity → automate; minimal management

**Consolidation:** Fewer, deeper supplier relationships deliver more cost reduction than many transactional ones.

## Four Types of Collaboration (from Ch 16 in cost context)

| Type | Cost Reduction Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **A: One-way data exchange** | Supplier uses POS data to reduce forecast error → less safety stock → lower cost |
| **B: Two-way data exchange** | Retailer uses supplier capacity data to plan promotions better → fewer emergency orders |
| **C: Cooperative (VMI/CPFR)** | Supplier manages replenishment → fewer stockouts → less emergency replenishment cost |
| **D: Cognitive (joint design)** | Co-design product for supply chain efficiency → reduce packaging cost, handling cost |

---

## Joint Rationalisation — Customer/Supplier Base

Steps to rationalise:
1. **Spend analysis** — what % of spend goes to how many suppliers?
2. **Pareto:** Top 20% of suppliers typically = 80% of spend → focus there
3. **Capability assessment** — which suppliers can/will collaborate?
4. **Consolidation plan** — move volume to fewer, better suppliers
5. **Transition plan** — manage supply risk during consolidation
