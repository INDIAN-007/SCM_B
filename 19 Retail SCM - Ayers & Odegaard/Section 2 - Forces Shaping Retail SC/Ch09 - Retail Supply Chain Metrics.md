## The Metrics Problem

Most companies measure supply chain performance with too many metrics, misaligned metrics, or metrics that don't connect to financial results. Ayers & Odegaard identify three levels:

| Level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Top-tier** | Assessment metrics | Tied to company strategy and financial performance |
| **Mid-tier** | Process metrics | Measure cross-functional or cross-company processes |
| **Ground-level** | Activity metrics | Measure individual activities (picking accuracy, truck utilisation) |

**Problem:** Most companies only have ground-level metrics. They don't link to strategy.

## The Driving Force Framework (Robert)

One of 10 driving forces is the "strategic heartbeat" of every company. The driving force determines which SC metrics matter most:

| # | Driving Force | Examples | SC Improvement Objective |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Product/service concept | GM, Boeing, IBM | Assure material support for product improvements |
| 2 | User/customer class | P&G for households, J&J for doctors | Design chains for tailored offerings to target users |
| 3 | Market type/category | Disney for families | Design chains to deliver product categories to target market |
| 4 | Production capability | Manufacturers | Optimise plant utilisation and output |
| 5 | Technology | Intel, Qualcomm | Secure supply of specialist components and materials |
| 6 | Sales/marketing method | Direct sellers, MLM | Support sales force; manage channel inventory |
| 7 | Distribution method | FedEx, department stores | Reduce cost; ensure product constraints met (e.g., cold chain) |
| 8 | Natural resources | Oil, mining | Improve logistics between source and conversion |
| 9 | Size/growth | Growth-driven conglomerates | Consolidate; seek economies of scale |
| 10 | Return/profit | Private equity, hedge fund-owned | Portfolio management; asset efficiency |

> **Rule:** If a company has no SC project supporting its driving force, management should reconsider its strategy.

## SCOR Model (Referenced)

The Supply Chain Operations Reference (SCOR) model defines SC processes as:
- **SOURCE** — procurement and supplier management
- **MAKE** — manufacturing/transformation
- **DELIVER** — order management, logistics, DC operations
- **RETURN** — reverse logistics
- **ENABLE** — planning, performance management, data management

SCOR has 100+ metrics — not all should be used. Select based on driving force.

## Three-Tier Metric Framework

### Service Metrics (How well do we serve?)
| Metric | Description |
|---|---|
| Perfect Order Rate | % of orders with no errors across all dimensions |
| On-Time In-Full (OTIF) | Delivered on time AND in full quantity |
| Shelf Availability | % time product is on shelf |
| Fill Rate | % of order lines filled completely |

### Operating Metrics (How efficient are we?)
| Metric | Description |
|---|---|
| Inventory Turns | COGS / Avg Inventory |
| Cash-to-Cash Cycle | Days from paying supplier to collecting from customer |
| Order Cycle Time | Time from order to delivery |
| Supply Chain Cycle Time | Time to fulfil an order if starting from zero stock |

### Financial Metrics (How profitable are we?)
| Metric | Description |
|---|---|
| GMROI | Gross Margin Return on Inventory Investment |
| Return on SC Assets | SC profit / SC asset base |
| SC Cost as % of Revenue | Total SC cost / Net sales |
| Total Supply Chain Cost | All costs from source to customer |

## Metric Alignment Principle

> A supply chain metric that doesn't connect to the income statement or balance sheet is a vanity metric.

**How to test alignment:**
1. Take any SC metric (e.g., picking accuracy)
2. Ask: How does a 1% change in this metric affect gross margin, operating cost, or inventory?
3. If you can't answer → the metric may not be worth tracking at that level
