## What Is an Activity System?

An **Activity System** maps the strategic choices a company makes and the activities that reinforce them. Developed by Michael Porter, it is used in this book to design supply chains that are consistent with strategy.

Three steps:
1. **Make choices and develop themes** — what strategic position does the company take?
2. **Define activities** — what specific activities deliver on that position?
3. **Draw links** — how do activities reinforce each other?

---

## IKEA Activity System Example

IKEA's strategic position: **Offer wide range of well-designed, functional furniture at prices so low that as many people as possible can afford them.**

### Strategic Themes:
- Self-service customer (no sales staff)
- Flat-pack design (customer assembles at home)
- Own design (not sourced from third parties)
- Warehouse-showroom format (store IS the warehouse)

### Activity Map:

| Activity | Links To |
|---|---|
| **Flat-pack design** | Reduces transport cost (more units per truck); reduces storage space; enables self-assembly |
| **In-store warehouse** | Eliminates distributor; lower cost; customer takes stock home = no last-mile delivery |
| **Large store format** | Economies of scale; full range in one location |
| **Global sourcing** | Low-cost manufacturing; consistent quality via own design |
| **Customer self-service** | Lowers staff cost; speeds up purchase; requires clear in-store navigation |
| **Restaurant / play area** | Extends dwell time; increases basket size |

**The key:** Every activity reinforces every other. This creates strategic fit that is hard to copy.

---

## Activity System → Supply Chain Design

The activity system defines what the supply chain must do:

| IKEA Activity System Requirement | SC Design Response |
|---|---|
| Flat-pack → needs precise BOM per product | Detailed product engineering + supplier spec compliance |
| Low price → needs low-cost global sourcing | Global supplier base; long-term contracts; volume leverage |
| In-store availability → needs reliable DC-to-store replenishment | Hub-and-spoke DC network; high fill rate to stores |
| Wide range → needs large SKU management capability | Advanced WMS; ABC analysis; SKU rationalisation |

## How to Build an Activity System for Your Business

1. **Define your strategic position in 1–2 sentences** (the "what we stand for")
2. **Identify 4–6 strategic themes** (the high-level choices that support the position)
3. **List activities** that deliver on each theme
4. **Draw links** between activities that reinforce each other
5. **Identify SC implications** for each linked activity cluster

## Enabling Processes vs Producing Processes

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Producing processes** | Directly create or deliver the product | Manufacturing, picking, packing, delivery |
| **Enabling processes** | Support producing processes | Demand planning, inventory management, IT, HR, finance |

**SC implication:** Enabling processes are often under-resourced. Demand planning is an enabler that directly determines how well every producing process operates.
