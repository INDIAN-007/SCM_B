# Ch 16 — Collaboration with Supply Chain Partners

> **Source:** Ayers & Odegaard, Chapter 16
> **Tags:** #retail #collaboration #partnership #Ayers

---

## Why Collaboration Is an "Unnatural Act"

Companies naturally optimise for their own interests. Sharing forecasts, inventory data, and plans with trading partners feels like giving away competitive intelligence. Yet this is exactly what Stage 3 SCM requires.

> "Forging supply chain partnerships with trading partners up and down the supply chain. This is an 'unnatural act' but increasingly a competitive must."

---

## Supply Chain Roles — Broader, Fewer

Modern SC roles are becoming:
- **Broader:** One person manages a full category SC, not just one function
- **Fewer:** Automation and data systems handle transactional tasks; humans focus on exceptions and relationships

---

## Collaboration Landscape

| Dimension | Transactional | Collaborative | Strategic Partnership |
|---|---|---|---|
| Data sharing | Purchase orders only | Forecasts, inventory levels | POS data, cost structures, NPD plans |
| Planning | Independent | Shared S&OP | Joint business planning |
| Risk/reward | Each party bears own risk | Some sharing | Shared risk/reward contracts |
| Duration | Spot / short-term | 1–3 year contracts | Multi-year strategic agreement |
| Investment | None | Limited | Dedicated resources, joint systems |

---

## Core Competency Principle

Before collaborating, each party must know their core competency:
- What does your company do better than anyone else?
- What should you outsource to a partner who does it better?

**Implication:** Collaboration only works when each party brings distinct value. If both partners do the same thing, there's no basis for partnership.

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Partnership Purpose** | Why the partnership exists; what customer value it creates |
| **Partnership Direction** | Who leads; who follows; how decisions are made |

## Four Types of Collaboration

| Type | Direction | Scope | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Type A: One-way data exchange** | One party shares data | POS data to supplier | Retailer shares weekly sales to supplier |
| **Type B: Two-way data exchange** | Both parties share data | Mutual visibility | Retailer shares POS; supplier shares inventory + capacity |
| **Type C: Cooperative collaboration** | Joint processes | CPFR, VMI | Joint demand plan; supplier manages replenishment |
| **Type D: Cognitive collaboration** | Joint strategy and design | Innovation partnerships | Co-develop products; jointly design the supply chain |

---

## Organising a Partnership

Steps to build a productive SC partnership:
1. **Define purpose** — what specific SC problem does this partnership solve?
2. **Map current process** — document as-is flows, responsibilities, pain points
3. **Agree on shared metrics** — what does success look like for both parties?
4. **Assign roles** — who does what in the new model?
5. **Build the technology connection** — EDI, portal, API, or shared platform
6. **Pilot** — start with a single category or product line
7. **Governance** — monthly review meeting; clear escalation path
