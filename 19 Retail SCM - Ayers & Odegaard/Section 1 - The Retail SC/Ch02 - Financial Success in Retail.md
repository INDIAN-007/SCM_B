## Why Finance Matters in SCM

Every SCM decision — how much inventory to hold, what lead time to accept, where to warehouse — has a direct impact on the income statement and balance sheet. Supply chain managers who understand retail finance make better decisions.

---

## Retail Income Statement — Two Archetypes

| Line Item | Discounter | High-End Retailer |
|---|---|---|
| Net Sales | $2,000M | $35M |
| Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) | 79% of sales | 33% of sales |
| **Gross Margin** | **21%** | **67%** |
| Operating Expenses | 15% of sales | 83% of sales |
| **Operating Profit** | **6%** | **~-16% or profitable based on service premium** |

**Takeaway:** Discounters win on volume and low cost; high-end retailers win on margin per unit. The supply chain design must match the model.

## Retail Balance Sheet — Key Items for SCM

| Asset | SCM Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Accounts Receivable** | Credit extended to B2B customers |
| **Inventory** | #1 SCM lever — reduces working capital when turned faster |
| **Cash** | Freed when inventory turns improve |
| **Fixed Assets** | Warehouses, stores, DC network |

| Liability | SCM Relevance |
|---|---|
| **Accounts Payable** | Longer payment terms = more cash retained |
| **Short-term debt** | Finances working capital (often backed by inventory) |

## DuPont Strategic Profit Model

Developed by DuPont during WWI — still the standard retail financial framework:

```
                    Net Profit Margin
                         ×
                    Asset Turnover
                         =
                    Return on Assets (ROA)
                         ×
                    Financial Leverage
                         =
                    Return on Equity (ROE)
```

**SCM levers in the DuPont model:**
- **↑ Inventory turns** → ↑ Asset Turnover → ↑ ROA → ↑ ROE
- **↓ SC cost** → ↑ Net Profit Margin → ↑ ROA
- **↓ Inventory** → ↓ Total Assets → ↑ ROA

## Key Financial Ratios (Ch 2, Table 2.3)

| Ratio | Formula | Discounter | High-End |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Inventory Turns** | COGS / Inventory | 5.0× | 2.0× |
| **Inventory Days of Supply** | Inventory / (COGS/365) | 73 days | 183 days |
| **Receivable Days** | Receivables / (Net Sales/365) | 6 days | 121 days |
| **Payable Days** | Payables / (COGS/365) | 39 days | 255 days |
| **Cash-to-Cash Cycle** | Rec days + Inv days − Pay days | 40 days | 49 days |
| **Return on Equity** | Pretax Profit / Owners Equity | 15% | 25% |

**SCM insight:** High-End retailer has 255-day payable terms — essentially financing its inventory with supplier credit. This is a powerful cash flow tool, but strains supplier relationships.

## Merchandise Budgeting

The merchandise budget is the retailer's buying plan:
- Sets **how much money** to spend per category per period
- Does NOT specify exact SKU quantities (that's the buying decision)
- Links to replenishment model for staple vs fashion products

### Staple Products (Functional)
- Continuous demand; predictable
- Replenishment model: **Reorder Point system** (ROP-based)
- Safety stock calculated from forecast error + lead time variability
- Systems adjust for seasonality automatically

### Fashion/Innovative Products
- Short selling window; unpredictable demand
- One-time buy or test-and-repeat
- Replenishment: **Open-to-Buy** (OTB) model
- Residual stock managed with markdowns

### Merchandise Budget Formula
```
Planned Sales
+ Planned End-of-Month (EOM) Stock
- Planned Beginning-of-Month (BOM) Stock
= Planned Purchases (at retail)
÷ (1 + Markup %)
= Planned Purchases (at cost)
```

## Opportunities for SCM to Improve Retail Financials (Khokha)

1. **Better merchandising decisions** — right products = higher sell-through, less markdown
2. **Getting product on time** — especially for fashion; late delivery = missed selling window
3. **Reduce slow-moving stock** — ABC/FSN analysis + markdown cadence
4. **Share forecasts with suppliers** — reduces upstream safety stock, lowers cost
5. **Negotiate delivery schedules** — more frequent, smaller deliveries = lower inventory days
6. **Supplier-held inventory** — VMI / consignment shifts inventory risk upstream
