## Retail Supply Chain Echelons

| Echelon | Role | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Retailers** | Sell directly to end consumers | Walmart, Target, NTUC FairPrice, Phoon Huat |
| **Distributors / Wholesalers** | Buy from OEMs, sell to retailers | Li & Fung, McKesson, Metro C&C |
| **OEMs (Original Equipment Manufacturers)** | Design and manufacture finished goods | P&G, Unilever, Nike, Apple |
| **1st-Tier Suppliers** | Supply components/materials to OEMs | Packaging companies, ingredient suppliers |
| **2nd-Tier Suppliers** | Supply to 1st-tier suppliers | Raw material producers, commodity farmers |
| **Service Providers** | Support logistics, IT, finance | DHL, UPS, SAP, banks, insurance |

## Retail Formats (US Census Classification)

| Format | Characteristics | Inventory Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Mass Merchandiser** | High volume, low margin, broad SKU | Fast turns, lean inventory, cross-docking |
| **Specialty Retailer** | Narrow category, deep assortment | Higher inventory days, curated selection |
| **Discount/Off-Price** | Opportunistic buying, no replenishment | Buy-in, sell-through, no reorder |
| **Department Store** | Multi-category, service-focused | OTB-driven, seasonal |
| **Supermarket/Grocery** | FMCG, fresh, consumables | JIT fresh, ROP dry goods |
| **E-commerce Pure Play** | No physical store | FC-based, last-mile optimised |
| **Omni-channel** | Physical + digital | Unified inventory, ship-from-store |

## Driving Forces — What Determines SCM Priority?

Based on Robert's "Driving Force" framework (expanded in Ch 9):
- A **product-driven** retailer (Nike) → SCM supports product innovation and brand consistency
- A **customer-class driven** retailer (Johnson & Johnson for healthcare) → SCM tailored to specific channel
- A **distribution-method driven** retailer (FedEx, food wholesaler) → SCM IS the business

**Implication:** There is no single "best" supply chain design — it must match the driving force.
