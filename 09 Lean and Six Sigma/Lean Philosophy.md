# Lean Philosophy

> **Source:** Kuldeepak Ch.6
> **Tags:** #lean #philosophy #waste

---

## Definition

Lean is a philosophy and system focused on **eliminating waste** (muda) and creating value for the customer. Every activity that does not add value to the customer is considered waste.

The goal: more value with fewer resources — for the customer, company, and supply chain.

---

## Origins

- Developed by Toyota as **Toyota Production System (TPS)** after WWII
- Popularised globally through *The Machine That Changed the World* (Womack, Jones, Roos, 1990)
- Extended beyond manufacturing to logistics, healthcare, services, and supply chain

---

## Five Lean Principles

1. **Identify Value** — Define value from the customer's perspective; only customer-defined value matters
2. **Map the Value Stream** — Identify all steps in the value stream; eliminate non-value-adding steps
3. **Create Flow** — Make value-creating steps flow without interruption, waste, or rework
4. **Establish Pull** — Only produce/move what the customer (internal or external) actually needs
5. **Pursue Perfection** — Continuously improve; there is always waste to be eliminated

## Three Types of Waste (Japanese)

| Japanese | English | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Muda** | Waste | Any non-value-adding activity (8 types — TIMWOODS) |
| **Mura** | Unevenness | Variability / fluctuation in process or demand |
| **Muri** | Overburden | Unreasonable demands on people or machines |

---

## Lean in Supply Chain

- Lean supply chain = minimal inventory, maximum flow, no buffer for bad processes
- **JIT** is the operational manifestation of lean thinking in supply chain
- Lean forces quality improvement (no inventory buffer to hide defects)
- Lean works best when demand is relatively stable and predictable

---

## Lean vs Agile

| Dimension | Lean | Agile |
|---|---|---|
| Demand | Stable, predictable | Variable, unpredictable |
| Focus | Eliminate waste | Respond to uncertainty |
| Inventory | Minimal | Sufficient buffer |
| Production | Level, continuous | Flexible, variable |

Most modern supply chains use a **Leagile** approach: lean upstream (supply), agile downstream (customer-facing).
