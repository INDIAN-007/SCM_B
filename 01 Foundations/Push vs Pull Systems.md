# Push vs Pull Systems

> **Source:** Chopra Ch.1 · Kuldeepak Ch.1
> **Tags:** #foundations #strategy #inventory

---

## Push Systems

**Definition:** Production and distribution decisions are based on long-term forecasts. Goods are pushed downstream before demand is confirmed.

### Characteristics
- Driven by schedule / MRP / forecast
- Higher inventory levels
- Risk of overstock / understock
- Works well for stable, predictable demand
- Lower cost per unit (economies of scale in production)

### Examples
- FMCG companies: P&G, HUL, Godrej
- Consumer electronics: seasonal production batches
- Make-to-Stock (MTS) model

---

## Pull Systems

**Definition:** Production and distribution are triggered by actual customer demand. Nothing is produced until a demand signal is received.

### Characteristics
- Demand-driven, not forecast-driven
- Lower inventory levels
- Higher responsiveness
- Works well for variable, customised demand
- Higher cost per unit (smaller batches)

### Examples
- Dell (pre-2007 Build-to-Order model)
- Toyota Kanban production system
- Make-to-Order (MTO) model

---

## Push–Pull Boundary

In most real supply chains, there is a **push–pull boundary** — upstream stages use push, downstream use pull.

```
Supplier → [PUSH: forecast-driven] → Decoupling Point → [PULL: demand-driven] → Customer
```

The decoupling point is where **inventory buffers** forecast uncertainty and enables pull.

---

## Chopra's Cycle View vs Push/Pull View

| View | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cycle View** | SC processes divided into a series of cycles between each pair of stages (customer order, replenishment, manufacturing, procurement cycle) |
| **Push/Pull View** | SC processes divided into those performed in response to a customer order (pull) vs those performed in anticipation (push) |
