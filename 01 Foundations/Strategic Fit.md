# Strategic Fit

> **Source:** Chopra Ch.2
> **Tags:** #foundations #strategy #competitive

---

## Definition

**Strategic fit** requires that both the competitive strategy and the supply chain strategy of a company have **aligned goals** — consistency between the customer priorities that the competitive strategy hopes to satisfy and the capabilities the supply chain strategy aims to build.

---

## Why It Matters

A company may fail because of:
1. **Lack of strategic fit** — SC strategy conflicts with competitive strategy
2. **Execution gap** — processes and resources cannot deliver the designed SC strategy

**Example of misalignment:** Marketing promises wide variety quickly; distribution chooses slow, cheap transport to consolidate orders → customers get late deliveries → surplus destroyed.

---

## Three Steps to Achieve Strategic Fit (Chopra)

### Step 1 — Understand Customer and Supply Chain Uncertainty

Customer demand varies along:
- **Quantity per lot** (emergency repair = small, construction = large)
- **Response time tolerance** (emergency = short, planned = long)
- **Product variety required**
- **Service level required**
- **Price sensitivity**

The demand uncertainty spectrum runs from **highly predictable** to **highly uncertain**.

### Step 2 — Understand Supply Chain Capabilities

The implied uncertainty spectrum maps to a **responsiveness–efficiency frontier**:

| Efficient Supply Chain | Responsive Supply Chain |
|---|---|
| Lowest cost | Highest availability |
| Standardised products | Wide variety |
- Stable demand | Unpredictable demand |
| Long lead times OK | Short lead times required |

### Step 3 — Achieve Strategic Fit

Map the customer's position on the uncertainty spectrum to the matching supply chain capabilities.

```
Low Uncertainty → Efficient SC
High Uncertainty → Responsive SC
Mismatch → Restructure SC or change competitive strategy
```

## Examples

### Dell (1993–2006) — Perfect Strategic Fit
- Competitive strategy: Customised PCs at reasonable price
- SC strategy: Build-to-order, flexible assembly, responsive carriers
- Result: Revenue and profit growth unprecedented

### Dell (2007 onwards) — SC Adaptation
- Market shifted: customers wanted standard, lower-cost PCs
- Dell sold through Walmart, outsourced to Foxconn (low-cost)
- SC shifted from responsiveness to efficiency focus
- Maintained strategic fit through adaptation

### Seven-Eleven Japan — Virtuous Cycle
- Marketing: convenience, variety, nearby access
- Operations: high store density, very responsive replenishment
- IT infrastructure: tracks demand by time of day → changes merchandise mix
- Result: Store sales grew from 1B yen (1974) to 2.7T yen (2016)

### Walmart — Efficiency-Focused Fit
- Competitive strategy: reliable low-cost retailer
- SC: cross-docking, EDLP pricing, shared information with suppliers, owned fleet
- All 6 drivers aligned to efficiency + adequate responsiveness

---

## Expanding Strategic Fit Across the Supply Chain

Strategic fit cannot be achieved by one function alone — it must span the **entire value chain**:

```
New Product Dev → Marketing & Sales → Operations → Distribution → Service
```

All functions must align toward the same customer priority.
